9. Quantum Darwinism: How Objective Reality Arises

We all look at the same moon. The position of the moon I see matches the position of the moon you see, and we agree on this ‚Äúfact.‚Äù But the quantum world is filled with superposition and the uncertainty of measurement. How can an objective classical world‚Äîagreed upon by all observers‚Äîemerge from these strange quantum laws?

Quantum Darwinism provides a powerful answer to this. This theory explains that the Environment is not merely a ‚Äúdestroyer‚Äù that merely destroys quantum superposition, but rather an ‚Äúactive broadcaster‚Äù that selects specific information (classical information) from the system and replicates its copies into billions of pieces, spreading them widely throughout the surroundings. üìú

1. Fundamental Concepts

  • Environment-Induced Superselection (Einselection): A prerequisite of Quantum Darwinism. When a system (S) interacts with the environment (E), most quantum superposition states (e.g., \(|0\rangle + |1\rangle\)) are extremely unstable and collapse instantly (decoherence). However, there are special states that survive stably despite the interaction with the environment, and these are called the Pointer Basis.

    • (Example: The ‚Äúposition‚Äù state of a macroscopic object. We do not see an object that is ‚Äúhere and there.‚Äù)
    • The environment forces the system into one of these ‚ÄúPointer Basis‚Äù states. This is the process by which classical states are ‚Äúselected.‚Äù

  • Quantum Darwinism: While Einselection explains the ‚Äúselection‚Äù of classical states, Quantum Darwinism explains the process by which this selected information becomes ‚Äúobjectified.‚Äù The environment does not passively obtain information about the system‚Äôs pointer states (e.g., ‚Äúthe moon is there‚Äù) but redundantly encodes copies of this information into the countless independent pieces that constitute the environment (e.g., scattered photons).

  • Emergence of Objectivity: Objectivity means that ‚Äúmultiple observers can independently confirm information and reach consensus.‚Äù

    • According to Quantum Darwinism, multiple observers (Alice, Bob, Charlie‚Ä¶) sample different pieces of the environment (e.g., photons entering Alice‚Äôs eyes, photons entering Bob‚Äôs eyes‚Ä¶).
    • If information is extremely redundantly copied into the environment, each observer can obtain the complete (classical) information of the system by observing only a tiny part of the environment.
    • Therefore, they inevitably agree on the state of the system, and this ‚Äúagreed-upon fact‚Äù is exactly the objective classical reality. üåç

    Detailed Explanation: Difference Between Decoherence and Darwinism

    • Decoherence (Loss of Coherence): Answers the question, ‚ÄúWhy don‚Äôt I see superposition when I look at the system?‚Äù It is the phenomenon where the system‚Äôs coherence disappears from my perspective as the environment entangles with the system. (First-person perspective)
    • Quantum Darwinism (Objectivity): Answers the question, ‚ÄúWhy do we all see the same thing?‚Äù It is the phenomenon where the environment redundantly replicates system information, allowing multiple observers to access the same information. (Third-person perspective)

    Quantum Darwinism is a much stronger condition than decoherence and is essential for explaining the emergence of objectivity.

  • Information Plateau: A key experimental evidence of quantum Darwinism. Plotting the mutual information between the system (S) and a fragment of the environment (F), \(I(S:F)\), as a function of the fragment size (\(f\)), reveals a specific pattern.

    1. Initial Surge: Information about the system rapidly increases even with very small environmental fragments.
    2. Plateau: Once \(I(S:F)\) reaches the system‚Äôs classical information content (\(H(S)\)), further addition of environmental fragments no longer increases the information, resulting in a ‚Äúplateau‚Äù region.
    3. Meaning: This plateau indicates that most environmental fragments contain the same information redundantly.

  • Redundancy (\(R_\delta\)): A quantitative measure indicating how wide the information plateau is, i.e., how much information has been replicated. A high \(R\) value implies that numerous observers can independently arrive at the same conclusion.

2. Symbols and Key Equations

  • System (S), Environment (E), Fragment (F):
    • The entire environment E is divided into \(N\) independent parts: \(E = \bigotimes_{k=1}^N E_k\).
    • \(F\) is a subset of the environment composed of \(k\) fragments, with its size represented by the fraction \(f = k/N\).
  • Mutual Information:
    • \(I(S:F) = H(S) + H(F) - H(S,F)\)
    • \(H(\rho) = -\mathrm{Tr}(\rho \log \rho)\) is the von Neumann entropy.
    • \(I(S:F)\) quantifies ‚Äúhow much information about the system S can be obtained by measuring the environmental fragment F?‚Äù
  • Classical-Quantum State:
    • An ideal state with completed einselection and information recording has the following form: \(\rho_{SF} = \sum_s p_s |s\rangle\langle s|_S \otimes \rho_F^{(s)}\)
    • \(|s\rangle\): The pointer basis of the system (classical state, e.g., position ‚Äòhere‚Äô)
    • \(p_s\): Classical probability of being in that state
    • \(\rho_F^{(s)}\): When the system is in state \(|s\rangle\), the ‚Äòcopy‚Äô of information recorded in the environmental fragment F (quantum state)
  • The Plateau Signature:
    • If quantum Darwinism holds, the \(I(S:F)\) vs \(f\) graph exhibits a characteristic shape.
    • \(I(S:F)\) rapidly increases from \(f \to 0\) until it reaches \(H(S)\) (the system‚Äôs classical information content), and then remains nearly constant until \(f\) approaches 1.

    üí° Why does the plateau form at \(H(S)\)?

    In an ideal classical-quantum state, if the environmental records \(\rho_F^{(s)}\) and \(\rho_F^{(s')}\) for distinct system states \(s\) and \(s'\) are mutually orthogonal, we can perfectly distinguish \(s\) and \(s'\) by measuring \(F\).

    That is, knowing \(F\) is equivalent to knowing \(S\), so \(H(S|F) \approx 0\) (uncertainty of \(S\) given \(F\) is zero).

    Since mutual information is \(I(S:F) = H(S) - H(S|F)\), \(I(S:F) \approx H(S)\) results. The plateau forming at this value implies that the environmental fragment \(F\) contains all classical information about the system.

  • Redundancy \(R_\delta\):
    • \(f_\delta\): minimum fraction of the environment required to obtain \((1-\delta)\) of the system information (e.g., 99%).
    • \(R_\delta = 1 / f_\delta\).
    • (Example) If there are \(N=10^{20}\) photons, and only 100 of them (\(f_\delta = 100/10^{20}\)) are sufficient to obtain enough information, the redundancy is \(R_\delta = 10^{18}\), which is extremely large. This means that \(10^{18}\) different observers can simultaneously confirm the information.

3. Easy Examples (Examples with Deeper Insight)

  • Example 1: Scattered Photons (Birth of Objectivity)
    • S: Desk lamp (pointer state: on \(|1\rangle\), off \(|0\rangle\))
    • E: Numerous photons in the room (\(E_k\))
    • Interaction: When the lamp is on (\(|1\rangle\)), photons scatter and spread throughout the room (\(\rho_k^{(1)}\)), and when it is off (\(|0\rangle\)), photons do not scatter (\(\rho_k^{(0)}\)).
    • Darwinian: \(\rho_k^{(0)}\) and \(\rho_k^{(1)}\) are very different states (almost orthogonal).
    • Objectivity: Alice sees scattered photons from the left side of the room (F1), and Bob sees scattered photons from the right side (F2). Both can reach the same conclusion that ‚Äúthe lamp is on‚Äù with only a small number of photons. \(R_\delta\) is very large.
  • Example 2: Incomplete Records (Collapse of the Plateau)
    • Situation: Same as Example 1, but fog makes the photon records incomplete (\(\rho_k^{(0)}\) and \(\rho_k^{(1)}\) become similar, i.e., non-orthogonal).
    • Result: Alice cannot be certain whether the lamp is on or whether the fog is blurring the observation by looking at only a few photons. To be \((1-\delta)\) certain about the lamp state, many more photon fragments (\(f_\delta\)) must be collected.
    • Graph: The initial rise of the \(I(S:F)\) graph becomes much slower, the height of the plateau drops below \(H(S)\), and the redundancy \(R_\delta\) drops sharply.

4. Exercises

  1. Information Content of Perfect Records: Prove that the mutual information \(I(S:F)\) is equal to \(H(S)\) when the environmental records are perfectly orthogonal (\(\rho_F^{(s)} \perp \rho_F^{(s')}\)) for all \(s \neq s'\) in the ideal classical-quantum state \(\rho_{SF} = \sum_s p_s |s\rangle\langle s| \otimes \rho_F^{(s)}\).
  2. Redundancy Calculation: The classical information content of a system is \(H(S)=1\) bit. The environment consists of \(N=10^{10}\) pieces. If \(k=100\) pieces (\(f_\delta = 100/N\)) are required to obtain 99% of the information (\(\delta=0.01\)), calculate the redundancy \(R_\delta\).
  3. Decoherence vs. Objectivity: Explain the statement ‚ÄúDecoherence alone does not guarantee objectivity.‚Äù What additional conditions are needed for objectivity?
  4. Incomplete Records: Suppose an environment piece \(E_k\) incorrectly records the system state with probability \(\epsilon\) (error). Qualitatively explain the values \(I(S:E_k)\) would take when \(\epsilon=0\) (perfect record) and \(\epsilon=0.5\) (complete noise).
  5. Multiple Observers Agreement: When redundancy \(R_\delta\) is much greater than \(M\) (\(R_\delta \gg M\)), explain why the probability that \(M\) different observers measuring different environmental pieces reach consensus about the system is very high.

5. Explanation

  1. \(I(S:F) = H(S) - H(S|F)\). \(H(S|F)\) is the remaining uncertainty about \(S\) after measuring \(F\). If all \(\rho_F^{(s)}\) are orthogonal, measuring \(F\) allows determining \(s\) perfectly (100% probability). Thus, uncertainty about \(S\) is 0, i.e., \(H(S|F)=0\). Therefore, \(I(S:F) = H(S)\).
  2. \(f_\delta = k/N = 100 / 10^{10} = 10^{-8}\). Redundancy \(R_\delta = 1/f_\delta = 1 / 10^{-8} = 10^8\) (100 million). This means 100 million observers can reach consensus about the system state with 99% accuracy even though they observe different environmental pieces.
  3. Decoherence means a single observer cannot see the system‚Äôs superposition (\(\rho_S\)‚Äôs off-diagonal terms vanish). However, it does not guarantee that other observers see the same classical result. For objectivity, the environment must do more than entangle; it must redundantly copy the system‚Äôs pointer information to many pieces. This redundancy (\(R_\delta \gg 1\)) is the additional condition for objectivity.
  4. \(I(S:E_k)\) is the information content of one piece (\(E_k\)).
    • \(\epsilon=0\) (perfect record): \(E_k\) perfectly copies \(S\), so \(I(S:E_k) = H(S)\).
    • \(\epsilon=0.5\) (complete noise): \(E_k\)‚Äôs state is completely uncorrelated with \(S\)‚Äôs state (e.g., coin flip). Observing \(E_k\) gives no information about \(S\), so \(I(S:E_k) = 0\).
  5. \(R_\delta \gg M\) means the total number of redundant pieces with information is much larger than the number of observers \(M\). When \(M\) observers randomly sample pieces from the environment, the probability that all sampled pieces are non-overlapping and all contain ‚Äúgood‚Äù pieces (with 99% information) is very high. Thus, all observers access the same classical information and reach consensus.
